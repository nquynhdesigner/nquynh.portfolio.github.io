Anatomy Character

  • drawing, blogdesign
  • 15 Comments

The study of human anatomy, for artists, can be as simple and straightforward as learning about proportions and working from life, or as involved and complex as mastering an understanding of the skeletal, muscular, and surface structure of the human body.

In order to create characters that give immediate, strong impressions of who they are, some of the most effective tools a character designer can use are exaggeration and simplification. Without betraying the necessary structures for anatomy to feel solid and true, a designer can push and pull the size and shape of individual parts and the manner in which they relate to each other to build characters that are a step beyond reality without seeming false.

Below is another great simplification/exaggeration example from the game Team Fortress 2. The game overall uses a really appealing simplification of shapes that not only makes it easy to read characters in a split second but also just has a fun aesthetic feel to it. You know right away that this isn't supposed to be a gritty, realistic experience, but a fun, ridiculous, over-the-top one. Certain exaggerations push this cartoony feel: the massive hands and character-driven facial proportions, for instance. It's notable that both the hands and faces are also really simplified. There's a blocky straightforwardness about all the shapes in these normally detailed areas. This serves quite a few purposes, from lightening the load on the game engine to being easier to read from a distance to simply establishing a particular look associated with the game. Overall it is effective and expressive.

Finally, for a peak of simplification, I'd like to take a look at the Irish film, The Secret of Kells. The story was in large part driven by the making of the Book of Kells, an illuminated manuscript of great importance both artistically and historically. The visual style of the film took its cues from illuminated manuscript drawings - resulting in a highly stylized look. All the characters and many set-pieces are defined by contour lines that often meet in tangents or flow together. This creates a sense of flatness that corresponds to the graphic, illustrative style of manuscript painting.

While there is still a sense of human anatomy present, many of the characters are far more represented by simple abstract shapes. The character designers, however, were carefully selective about what to represent in more detail and how to represent anatomical characteristics in an abstract, flat-looking way. For instance, in the model sheet for the character Brother Aidan below: the design communicates the bony knuckles of an old man's hands, forehead wrinkles and smile lines. But it does it all in a contour-based manner that works mostly with very basic shapes.
